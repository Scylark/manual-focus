## The setup

MAAP is a premium cycling apparel brand. The market has a few specific dynamics that shape how SEO has to work:

- **High intent, low volume per term.** People searching for "merino base layer cycling" don't search often, but when they do, they're often very close to a purchase decision.
- **Brand-loyal category.** Cyclists pick a kit brand and stick. The job of SEO is partly to capture demand and partly to *introduce* the brand to riders who haven't heard of you yet.
- **Authority-driven.** Cycling media (Cyclingnews, GCN, Escape Collective, Bicycling) is highly trusted. Featured-in editorial is worth more than ad spend.
- **Crowded long tail.** Every product category — bib shorts, jerseys, gilets, gravel kit — has a lot of competitors making technically similar product. Differentiation happens at the brand and category-narrative layer.
- **Global market with local nuance.** Strong markets in AU, US, UK, EU, JP. The customer is similar but the journey isn't identical.

That shaped the strategy.

## The three layers

We treated SEO as three layered jobs, each with its own metric.

**Layer one: brand-search ownership.** Anyone searching "MAAP", "MAAP bib shorts", "MAAP jersey" should land on a page we control, ranking number one, with rich product previews and review schema. This is the easiest and the most overlooked. The job is to make sure that any rider who has heard of MAAP and is checking the brand out finds the strongest possible introduction.

What this looks like in practice: brand name + category combinations all owned with dedicated pages, knowledge panel claimed and maintained, sitelinks structured to surface the most-considered products, review schema on every product, FAQ schema on category pages, structured pricing data.

**Layer two: category-level discovery.** Cyclists searching for the *thing* before they know which brand — "best merino base layer", "lightweight gravel jersey", "winter bib tights" — should encounter MAAP as a credible answer. This is where the long-form editorial work goes.

The shape: deep category pieces ("Choosing a winter bib short: a guide"), product comparisons (without naming competitors negatively — the brand voice has to hold), occasion-based guides ("Kit for sub-zero winter riding"), and gear roundups built by the in-house team rather than scraped. The pieces are written to be the genuine best answer to the query, with first-party expertise, real testing experience and original photography. AEO frame: each piece is structured so an LLM can pull a usable quote.

**Layer three: editorial and ambassador-driven authority.** The pieces that earn the link, the citation and the brand awareness lift. These don't always rank directly. They drive the topical authority that makes everything else rank better.

Riders profiles, race coverage, training pieces written by actual coaches, route guides, gravel and adventure narrative content. The goal is to be a publication-quality cycling editorial property in its own right, where the brand sits behind the content. The links and the topic associations come naturally when the work is actually good.

## Technical SEO — the boring layer that compounds

Premium brands almost always under-invest here. The work isn't sexy but the leverage is huge.

The audit list we ran (and revisit quarterly):

- **Crawl budget hygiene.** Faceted navigation locked down so Google isn't wasting crawl on permutation pages that don't sell anything. `noindex,follow` on filtered URLs, canonical handling tight.
- **International setup.** `hreflang` configured cleanly across all market versions. Each country site treated as a distinct authority surface, not a copy. Localised content where it matters (sizing, climate, regulations), shared where it doesn't.
- **Page speed.** Image optimisation pipeline (modern formats, responsive sizes, lazy loading by default). CLS under control. LCP under 2.5s on mobile across the catalogue.
- **Schema density.** Product, review, FAQ, breadcrumb, organisation, video. The richer the schema, the more surface area in the SERP and the better the LLM citations.
- **Site architecture.** Category → subcategory → product depth max three clicks. Internal linking patterns reviewed quarterly to push authority where you want it.
- **Search Console signal.** Coverage issues triaged within 72 hours of detection. Lost pages investigated, not ignored.

The technical work is where most cycling brands silently bleed. They have great product, decent content, and a site architecture that makes crawlers' jobs hard. Fix that and the rest of the work compounds.

## Content engine cadence

The pace that produces compounding results:

- **Weekly:** one long-form editorial or guide piece. Real expertise, original photography, structured for both human reading and AEO.
- **Monthly:** one major category piece (a buyer's guide, a deep how-to, a flagship comparison).
- **Quarterly:** one campaign piece tied to product launch or seasonal moment, designed to earn coverage.
- **Continuous:** product page copy review, schema updates, internal link maintenance.

That cadence held with a small team because the workflow uses AI for the front-end research and structuring, with senior editorial and rider expertise running the angle and the testing. Same team would have produced maybe a third the volume without the AI workflow. Same quality.

## What we'd do again

A few moves that landed disproportionately:

- **Owned the "what should I wear for X" surface.** A library of occasion-based kit guides ("kit for 0–5°C", "kit for sub-2-hour gravel races", "kit for the alps in July") that ranked broadly and converted hard. People search by weather and occasion more than they search by product type.
- **Built ambassador editorial deeply.** Long-form profiles of riders associated with the brand, in their voice. These pieces earn links from cycling media because they're real, and they build the topical authority that lifts everything around them.
- **Treated returns and sizing as SEO content.** "How does MAAP fit?" gets searched a lot. Most brands have a sizing chart and call it done. We built that into a proper editorial piece with rider input, comparison context, photography. Reduced returns, lifted conversion, ranked first.
- **Killed product pages that didn't earn their keep.** Discontinued lines got redirected up to category, not left to rot. Avoided cannibalisation and tightened the architecture.

## What we'd do differently

A few things we'd reset if we started today:

- **More structured data, earlier.** We were late to AEO. The brands building for LLM citation now are the brands ranking next year.
- **Quicker on video.** YouTube SEO compounds. Embedded video on category pages compounds. The team capacity to produce both was the bottleneck.
- **Earlier multilingual.** The non-English markets were left simpler than they should have been. Each is a top-tier market in its own right.

## The principle that ran underneath all of it

Be the genuine best answer for the queries that matter. Make every signal — content, schema, links, internal structure, speed — reinforce that you are the strongest answer. Don't try to game it. Compete on actual quality and let the technical SEO surface the work.

That's the thing that travels. Brand, category, market — the principle works the same. Premium cycling apparel, premium AI tooling, premium B2B SaaS. The compounding comes from being right, repeatedly, in the way the searcher needs the answer.
