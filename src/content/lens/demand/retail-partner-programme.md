## The brief

Endurance brands sell two ways: direct (DTC website, brand stores)
and through partners (independent bike shops, running stores,
multisport retailers, larger chains). The brands that scale do
both. The brands that struggle treat partners as a freight channel
— ship the product, hope it sells, blame the shop when it doesn't.

This playbook is the operational discipline for the partner side.
A tier menu defining what kind of partner gets what kind of support,
a sell-in kit that gives partners the language and assets to actually
sell the brand, a training cadence that keeps shop staff
knowledgeable, and a sell-through measurement framework that
distinguishes good partners from inventory dumping grounds.

It does not replace the relationship work. Partner accounts are
won and held by salespeople. The playbook is the supporting
infrastructure around that relationship.

## The pipeline

Six phases.

**Phase 1 — Partner tier definition.** Three tiers, each with
explicit support and expectations:

- **Tier A — Concept partner.** Carries the full range, dedicates
  shop space, trains staff, hosts brand events. Receives: best
  margins, exclusive products, on-site training, marketing co-fund.
  Typical: 10–30 accounts globally for a growth-stage brand.
- **Tier B — Range partner.** Carries the core range, trains staff,
  represents the brand fairly to walk-in customers. Receives:
  standard margins, sell-in kit, virtual training. Most accounts
  live here.
- **Tier C — Stocking partner.** Carries select SKUs, no dedicated
  brand commitment. Receives: standard product access, basic
  materials. Low support cost, low expectations.

Each partner is assigned a tier based on annual sell-through, brand
representation quality, training engagement, and geographic
importance. Tier moves up/down based on annual review.

**Phase 2 — Sell-in kit.** The materials a shop owner reads to
decide whether to stock the brand:

- **One-page positioning** — what the brand is, who it's for, what
  walk-in customer they should recommend it to
- **Range overview** — every SKU, the audience it serves, the
  technology / craft that makes it distinct
- **Margin and terms** — clear, no hidden steps. Shops have been
  burnt; clarity is the differentiator.
- **Best-sellers** — actual sell-through data from comparable shops.
  Don't ask shops to bet on a hunch.
- **Display kit** — POS materials, fixture inserts, product cards.
  Shop floor space is contested; the brand earns it with quality
  assets.
- **First-90-days plan** — what the brand will do in the first
  quarter of the partnership to drive sell-through (event, training
  visit, customer-acquisition campaign in the local market)

The sell-in kit is read by shop owners with thirty other brands
also pitching. Brevity and substance win.

**Phase 3 — Training cadence.** Shop staff are the brand's mouth
in the store. Untrained staff become the cheapest competitor's
salesperson by default. The cadence:

- **Onboarding** — within 30 days of partner sign-on, a 90-minute
  product-knowledge session for shop staff. In-person where feasible,
  Zoom otherwise. Recorded for later hires.
- **New-product launches** — every major launch, a 30-minute deck +
  product walk-through delivered to all Tier A and B partners
- **Seasonal refresher** — at the start of each season, a 45-minute
  session covering the season's range, the audience it serves,
  fitting tips, common customer questions
- **On-demand library** — a portal (Notion, Workdrive, whatever)
  with every recorded session, product spec sheet, fitting guide,
  warranty FAQ

Training engagement is tracked. A Tier B partner that hasn't
attended training in two seasons becomes Tier C at the annual review.

**Phase 4 — Sell-through measurement.** What the brand actually
tracks per partner per quarter:

- **Sell-through ratio** — units sold from shop floor / units
  shipped to shop. Healthy is 70%+ within the season.
- **Range coverage** — which SKUs the partner actually carries and
  sells. Partners stocking only the discount lines aren't
  representing the brand.
- **Customer reviews mentioning the partner** — proxy for whether
  the partner is selling the brand well or just transactionally
- **Training engagement** — staff hours invested in brand training
- **Marketing co-investment** — does the partner co-fund local
  campaigns?

Each partner gets a scorecard quarterly. The scorecard is shared
with the partner; the partner gets a chance to respond before
tier changes.

**Phase 5 — Annual review and tier adjustment.** Once a year, every
partner gets reviewed against the scorecard. Decisions: promote
tier (with attendant increased support), maintain, demote, or end
the relationship.

End-the-relationship is a real outcome and shouldn't be avoided
when the data supports it. Bad partners drag the brand down in
their local market.

**Phase 6 — Brand-owned account in partner regions.** Even with
strong partners, the brand maintains some direct presence in each
key region — a DTC online experience for that geography, occasional
brand events in the city. This isn't competition with partners; it's
brand visibility that drives traffic to partners.

Partners initially resist this; the brands that explain it well
(direct = top-of-funnel; partner = consideration + close)
find partners come to appreciate the brand-level demand it
generates.

## The capability boundary

What AI helps with:

- **Drafting** the sell-in kit, training decks, partner emails,
  scorecards
- **Personalised partner communication** at scale (per-partner
  emails referencing their actual sell-through data)
- **Sell-through analysis** — surface patterns across partners,
  predict at-risk accounts
- **Training content generation** — product knowledge briefs,
  fitting guides, customer-question answers
- **Translation** — partner kits in multiple languages for
  international rollout

What AI doesn't help with:

- **The relationship itself.** Partner trust is built by salespeople
  showing up. AI augments the salesperson; it doesn't replace them.
- **The decision to drop a partner.** That's a human judgement call.
- **In-person training.** Tier A partners need humans in the shop.

## The eval harness

**Eval P1 — Sell-through health.** 70%+ within-season sell-through
ratio across Tier B+ partners. Below that, the channel is leaking
inventory that becomes off-season clearance pressure.

**Eval P2 — Training engagement.** 80%+ of Tier A and B partners
attend at least one training session per quarter. Below that, the
brand is being sold by uninformed staff.

**Eval P3 — Tier honesty.** Tier moves at annual review should
follow the data, not the relationship comfort. If no partners moved
tiers in a year, the review wasn't a real review.

**Eval P4 — Coverage breadth.** Geographic coverage in priority
markets. Gaps flagged for partner recruitment.

## The failure modes

**Treating all partners as the same.** Tier menu exists for a
reason. A bike shop in Bristol with 800 keen customers is not the
same as a sport chain stocking 40 brands.

**Sell-in kit as a deck.** Shops don't have time for a 60-slide
brand deck. The sell-in kit is one-page positioning + the data the
shop owner needs to make a stocking decision.

**Training that gets ignored.** Boring product-spec walkthroughs
have zero attendance. Training that combines product knowledge
with selling tactics ("when a customer says X, here's the response
that converts") gets engagement.

**Sell-through measured only by revenue.** Revenue without sell-
through ratio masks dumping. A partner that bought £200k of inventory
but only sold £80k of it is a problem disguised as a strong account.

**Avoiding the demote conversation.** Partners that have slid don't
self-correct without the conversation. Have it. The relationship
survives if the conversation is data-led and respectful.

## The pattern in practice

Illustrative scenarios — common shapes retail partner programmes take. Specifics are illustrative; the patterns repeat.

**Premium cycling brand, scale-stage — the tier reckoning.** A
brand with 200+ stocking partners and no way to tell which are
performing. Installing the tier-and-scorecard system and running
one honest annual review typically splits the base into a small
top tier, a larger middle, a long tail of low-performance partners,
and a meaningful number of relationships to end. Total revenue
usually holds; cost-to-serve drops materially; sell-through ratio
across the remaining base improves within two seasons.

**Trail running brand, growth-stage — the training cadence
finding.** A brand treating all running stores identically. The
training cadence reveals that a substantial fraction of partners
have never had a staff member attend a session. Restructuring
around the tier definitions and investing heavily in a small group
of Tier A partners typically lifts comparable sales in those
shops materially year-on-year.

**Multi-sport brand — the skipped-review failure.** A common
pattern: the brand executes phases 1–4 cleanly but skips Phase 5 —
the annual review never happens because nobody wants to make the
demote calls. Partners drift to Tier C-quality with Tier A-cost-
to-serve and the programme decays. The framework only works if
the discipline of the annual review is held.
