## The brief

Most endurance brands publish two flavours of content: gear stories
(self-interested) and athlete features (sponsorship-driven). Both
serve the brand. Neither serves the audience's actual need, which is
practical help getting fitter, faster, more durable, more consistent.
Brands that fill that gap earn audience trust the way no product
campaign can.

This playbook builds the training-content engine. A year-long
editorial series rooted in the brand's coaching POV, drafted with
the same eval gates as commercial content, reviewed by a credible
coach or athlete before publication. The brand becomes the place
the audience returns to for practical work, not just product news.

It does not require the brand to employ a head coach. It does
require access to one credible voice — sponsored athlete, advisor,
founder if they coach, partner coaching practice — who reviews the
content before publish.

## The pipeline

Five phases.

**Phase 1 — Coaching POV.** The brand articulates its perspective in
1,500–2,500 words. What does it believe about training that's
distinctive? High-volume aerobic base or polarised? Strength
adjunct or running-only? Periodised peak or year-round consistency?
The POV is the editorial spine. Without it, the content is generic.

Most brands struggle here because they haven't been forced to
articulate. The pipeline pauses and runs a 90-minute working
session with the brand's coaching voice to extract the POV before
proceeding.

**Phase 2 — Topic taxonomy.** From the POV, generate the topic tree:

- **Foundations** — base building, easy running, aerobic discipline,
  the long ride, swim drills (10–15 pieces)
- **Building blocks** — interval structures, tempo work, threshold,
  hill repeats (12–18 pieces)
- **Recovery and adaptation** — sleep, fuelling, off-season, easy-
  day discipline, the recovery week (10–12 pieces)
- **Mental work** — pacing, race-day execution, suffering, focus,
  the long block (8–10 pieces)
- **Practical** — gear choices for training (not selling), data
  reading, training peaks, plateau diagnosis (8–10 pieces)
- **Seasonal** — winter, spring, peak, taper, off-season cycles per
  discipline (6–8 pieces)

Total: 50–70 pieces across a year. Most brands ship 12. The volume
gap is the opportunity.

**Phase 3 — Drafting pipeline.** Every piece runs through eval-gated-
drafting (the standard pipeline) with three extensions:

- **Coaching-truth gate** — the piece is read against the brand's
  POV doc. Pieces that contradict the POV without explicit
  acknowledgement get flagged. Pieces that restate the POV without
  adding signal get flagged.
- **Practical-application gate** — every piece must include at least
  one concrete "if you're [audience tier], try this" prescription.
  Generic principles without application get rejected.
- **Credibility-reference gate** — claims about training physiology
  reference established research or named coaching voices. Hand-
  wavy "studies have shown" gets rejected; "Seiler 2010" with the
  actual citation gets through.

**Phase 4 — Expert review.** Every piece reviewed by the brand's
credible coaching voice before publish. The reviewer's edits feed
back into the drafting pipeline as training data — the next month's
drafts are sharper because the reviewer has shaped the model's
defaults.

**Phase 5 — Distribution and re-use.** Each piece outputs:

- Long-form for blog / email
- Mid-form for LinkedIn newsletter
- Short-form social cuts (carousel post, video script, Twitter / X
  thread)
- Audio version for podcast feed (optional)
- Reference page in the brand's training resource library

A single 2,000-word piece typically produces 4–7 channel outputs
across a launch week.

## The capability boundary

What AI does well here:

- **Drafting from the POV doc + topic brief** — production-ready with
  the gates above
- **Citation lookup and verification** — production-with-guardrails;
  every cited study fact-checked
- **Channel-native repurposing** — production-ready via the social-
  content-factory pipeline
- **Audio versions** — voice synthesis works for podcast-style
  reading. Disclose; don't pretend it's a human voice.

What AI does NOT do well:

- **Coaching judgement.** The brand's credible coach makes the
  judgement calls. AI drafts; humans approve.
- **Original research.** Don't let the model invent training-physiology
  claims. Anchor in established work.
- **Athlete-anecdote fabrication.** "I once had a runner who..." — if
  the brand's coach actually said it, use it. If they didn't, don't.

## The eval harness

**Eval T1 — POV consistency.** Sample 20 published pieces quarterly.
Reviewer scores each against the POV (1–5). Mean score should hold
above 4.0. Lower means the pipeline is drifting from the brand's
voice.

**Eval T2 — Citation accuracy.** Random-sample 5 published pieces;
verify every citation against the actual source. Acceptance: 100%.
A single fabricated study gets noticed by the audience that reads
training content closely.

**Eval T3 — Practical-application rate.** Every piece passes the
practical-application gate before publish. Audit monthly.

**Eval T4 — Audience response.** Quarterly: engagement on training
content vs gear / athlete content. Training content should out-
engage gear content within 6 months. If it doesn't, the brand's
POV isn't distinctive or the practical-application gate is too
soft.

## The failure modes

**No POV.** Brand wants to publish training content without
articulating what it actually believes. The pipeline produces
generic Runner's-World-style work that any brand could have
written. Refuse to start until the POV exists.

**Expert review without authority.** The reviewer needs the
credibility to push back on draft language. If the brand uses a
junior coach who defers to whatever the AI drafted, the gate
isn't a gate.

**Citation laundering.** Brand wants to look researched but doesn't
have the patience. The pipeline drafts, citations get added by
search, citations turn out to misrepresent the original studies.
Always read the cited source before publishing the citation.

**Repurposing kills the substance.** A 2,000-word piece compressed
to a 280-character tweet loses the "practical application" detail
that made the long form useful. Repurposing should keep one
specific applicable detail per channel, not just the headline.

**Self-cannibalisation.** Brand publishes 50 pieces a year but they
overlap heavily (three pieces on "easy running") and undersell the
breadth. The topic taxonomy is the discipline; cover the breadth
before doubling up.

## The pattern in practice

Illustrative scenarios — common shapes a training-content engine takes. Specifics are illustrative; the patterns repeat.

**Cycling brand, scale-stage — the credible-coach unlock.** A brand
publishing a couple of training pieces a month, ad-hoc. Installing
the engine with a sponsored cycling coach as the credible voice
typically lifts output to several pieces a month with expert-review
on every one. Email open rates on training content run a multiple
of open rates on gear content; newsletter sub-to-customer
conversion from training-content readers runs well above the
brand average.

**Trail running brand, growth-stage — the founder-as-coach.** A
brand whose founder is a practicing ultrarunner. Extracting his
POV in a single working session, generating the topic tree, and
drafting against a founder-as-reviewer cadence typically makes
training content the single largest organic traffic source within
a year, overtaking gear content.

**Triathlon brand — the no-credible-voice failure.** A common
failure mode: a brand wanting training content without a credible
coaching voice on the team or under contract. Drafting against a
generic POV produces engagement indistinguishable from the brand's
gear content. The honest fix is signing a coach first. Engagement
follows. The lesson: the credible voice is non-negotiable.
