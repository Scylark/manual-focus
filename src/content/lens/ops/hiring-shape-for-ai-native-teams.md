## The brief

The hiring shape of a marketing team is changing faster than the standard job descriptions can keep up. "Content marketer" used to mean 80% writing, 20% strategy. Now in an AI-leveraged team, the same role is 30% strategy, 30% pipeline curation, 30% eval-and-edit, 10% drafting. The job has changed; the title and description usually haven't.

This playbook is the updated taxonomy. Four roles that map to where leverage actually sits in an AI-native marketing function. Interview signals that test for the right things (judgement, eval discipline, pipeline thinking) rather than the wrong things (volume output, tool familiarity). Trial-task packs that surface real competence in 4-6 hours of candidate time, not 20.

## The four roles

**Role A — Strategy operator.** Sets the brand position, defines the briefs, owns the success metrics. Their job is to be right about what to do, and to keep the team from drifting into busywork. 40% of comp budget on a senior team.

**Role B — Pipeline curator.** Owns the playbook library (this one or yours). Maintains rubrics, calibrates evals, retires pipelines that aren't earning their place, builds new ones from observed wins. The role most teams don't have and most need.

**Role C — Output editor.** The senior writer / designer / video producer. Reviews work that the pipelines produce. Owns brand voice and craft. Half the time of a pre-AI editor; the other half is now upstream in pipeline curation.

**Role D — Channel operator.** Ships, schedules, instruments, reports. The function's hands. Knows the platforms, the trackers, the schedulers. Roughly the same as the pre-AI version of the role.

What's been compressed: junior drafters. A pre-AI team of 8 was typically 4 drafters + 2 editors + 1 strategist + 1 channel operator. The AI-native version is 1 strategy operator + 1 pipeline curator + 2 editors + 1 channel operator. Same output, different shape.

## The interview signal

For each role, the interview is structured around what the role actually does, not generic competencies.

**Strategy operator interview signals:**
- Can articulate a metric-anchored hypothesis from a vague problem
- Reads a marketing brief and finds the missing-required-field gaps
- Names the failure modes of their own past work
- Distinguishes "interesting" from "important"

**Pipeline curator interview signals:**
- Can read a playbook (this one) and identify what would break for their last team
- Knows the difference between rubric-as-judge and model-as-judge
- Has seen at least one prompt fail and can describe why
- Calibrates own confidence (says "I don't know" when they don't)

**Output editor interview signals:**
- Can describe the brand's voice in observable patterns, not adjectives
- Diffs two drafts and explains what changed in voice terms
- Can write the voice rubric from a small corpus
- Pushes back on briefs that lack a hook

**Channel operator interview signals:**
- Knows the limits of the platforms they operate
- Has built a tracker / dashboard that a non-technical reader would use
- Can describe a holdout / incrementality test they've run
- Articulates what they don't measure and why

## The trial-task packs

A trial task is 4-6 hours of focused work that mirrors the role's actual day. For each role:

**Strategy operator trial.** A real (anonymised) brief from your function with the required fields removed. Candidate completes the brief and proposes the success metric. Scored on whether they identify the missing fields, whether their metric is concrete, whether their outcome statement is causal.

**Pipeline curator trial.** A working but suboptimal pipeline (we provide an example with this playbook). Candidate identifies the failure modes, proposes the eval rubric, recommends pipeline changes. Scored on diagnosis quality, not on rewriting the whole pipeline.

**Output editor trial.** A passing-the-gates draft from your function's pipeline. Candidate edits with the brand voice rubric. Scored on whether the edits move the rubric scores up, not on whether the candidate "improved" the draft per their personal taste.

**Channel operator trial.** A campaign brief that needs instrumentation, scheduling, and a one-week reporting view. Candidate ships a spec. Scored on whether the instrumentation actually answers the brief's metric question.

## The failure modes

**Hiring for tool familiarity.** Tool names change every six months. "Has used Notion AI" is not a signal. "Has built an eval rubric that worked" is. Filter the JD for tool-name requirements and replace with capability requirements.

**Trial tasks that aren't representative.** A 20-hour take-home is exploitative and selects for unemployed candidates. A 30-minute live exercise is too short to surface real competence. The 4-6 hour structured trial is the goldilocks zone — paid, scoped, mirrors the work.

**Mistaking output volume for output quality.** AI-native teams produce more output per head. Promoting on volume produces the wrong incentive — the next hire optimises for output rather than judgement. Measure on outcome correlation, not output rate.

**Senior roles end up doing junior work.** Without a pipeline curator role, the strategy operator ends up calibrating evals between strategy work. Without an output editor, the strategy operator ends up reviewing drafts. The taxonomy exists to keep each role doing its leveraged work; collapsing roles to "save headcount" usually saves headcount and destroys the leverage.

**Over-rotating to AI literacy.** Some hiring managers screen so aggressively for AI competence that they miss strong marketing operators who haven't yet had to use it. AI literacy is teachable in 6-12 weeks for a strong operator. Marketing judgement isn't teachable in 6-12 months. Hire for the harder thing.

## The receipts

**B2B SaaS, scale-stage.** Function had been 9 generalist content marketers. After two quarters of installing the role taxonomy and running 4 hires through the new trial packs, the function is 6 people in the four-role shape and shipping 70% more output that hits its briefs. Two of the original 9 transitioned into the new shape; the others moved to other teams or companies.

**D2C, growth-stage.** Brand was about to hire a 5th content marketer. We mapped the workload against the taxonomy and the function was missing a pipeline curator entirely. Brand hired the curator instead. Six months later, throughput was up 4x without the extra two heads they'd budgeted for. The pipeline curator's compensation paid for itself within the first quarter.
