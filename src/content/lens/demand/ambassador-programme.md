## The brief

The endurance categories are full of brands paying athletes for posts
that get sub-1% engagement, while the brands that quietly back the
right athletes at the right race build year-on-year audience trust.
The difference isn't budget — it's the discipline of selection,
contracts, content cadence, and performance review.

This playbook is that discipline. Athlete selection rubric that's
honest about what fit looks like. Contract template covering AI
augmentation explicitly (a 2025+ requirement). Monthly content
cadence that doesn't burn the athlete out or the audience into
numbness. Quarterly performance review that decides whether to
renew, expand, or end.

It does not tell you which athletes to sign. Brand and category fit
is a human call. It does give you the framework to make the call
honestly and to manage the relationship without it eating the
marketing team's week.

## The pipeline

Six phases.

**Phase 1 — Selection rubric.** Score candidate athletes against a
multi-dimensional rubric:

- **Result credibility** — actual race results in the brand's sport,
  at the level the audience cares about (0–10)
- **Audience alignment** — does the athlete's existing audience
  overlap with the brand's? Scrape their social, score the audience
  cohort against the brand's target segments (0–10)
- **Content credibility** — does the athlete write/film/talk like
  someone who's done the work? Sample their last 20 posts (0–10)
- **Visual rights cleanliness** — does the athlete already have
  exclusive deals that block this brand's category? Verify before
  conversation (binary pass/fail)
- **Personal alignment** — does the athlete's public stance on the
  things the brand cares about (doping, environment, gender parity)
  align? Read their public record (0–10)
- **Schedule realism** — does the athlete's race calendar make
  collaboration possible? Some athletes are away 200+ days a year
  (0–10)

The rubric is calibrated against athletes the brand has previously
worked with successfully and unsuccessfully. The model does the
audience-scrape and content-credibility passes. Humans make the
final score on the qualitative dimensions.

**Phase 2 — Contract template.** Standard ambassador contract plus
specific clauses for the 2025+ AI landscape:

- **AI augmentation rights.** Brand may use captured footage to
  produce variants via AI — environmental adaptations, time-of-day
  variants, social-cut adaptations. Brand may NOT produce generated
  content purporting to be the athlete in scenarios they didn't
  actually participate in. The line is "augmenting reality" vs
  "fabricating a moment."
- **Likeness boundaries.** Voice cloning prohibited without separate
  written consent. Face/body substitution into other contexts
  prohibited. Athlete name and likeness require explicit prior
  sign-off on each piece beyond standard captured-content uses.
- **AI synthetic content disclosure.** Brand commits to disclosing AI
  augmentation in any piece where a reasonable viewer might think
  the footage was filmed practically.
- **Sunset clause.** AI augmentation rights end 18 months after the
  contract ends. The brand can't keep producing variants five years
  on without a renewal.
- **Standard provisions.** Exclusivity bands, payment schedule,
  content delivery obligations, social posting requirements, race
  appearance commitments.

**Phase 3 — Onboarding shoot.** First 60 days after signing, the
brand runs a substantial shoot — products in athlete's hands, training
footage, environment variants, talking-head interviews, candid b-roll.
The shoot output is the archive the AI augmentation pipeline draws
from for the next 12 months. Plan for 2–3 days of capture, multiple
locations relevant to the athlete's sport.

**Phase 4 — Monthly content cadence.** Sustainable rhythm:

- **One major piece per quarter** — long-form film, magazine feature,
  podcast interview, sponsored event appearance
- **One mid-piece per month** — newsletter feature, blog interview,
  carousel post series, ride/run-along YouTube piece
- **Two lightweight pieces per month** — social post, story
  appearance, race-day acknowledgement, gear cameo
- **Zero forced posts.** Athletes posting because the contract says
  they must do four posts a month — the audience can smell it.

**Phase 5 — Performance instrumentation.** Track per-athlete
quarterly:

- Audience growth attributable to the athlete partnership
- Engagement on the athlete's brand-tagged content vs their non-
  branded posts
- Pipeline-qualified actions tied to athlete content (newsletter
  signups, store visits, race-week sales spikes)
- Athlete sentiment — how the athlete describes the partnership in
  interviews where the brand isn't paying for the message

**Phase 6 — Renewal review.** End of contract term, a structured
review against the original rubric. Score the actual outcome against
the expected one. Decision: renew with expanded scope, renew flat,
taper, or end gracefully. Document the call so the next signing has
the lessons.

## The capability boundary

Where AI helps:

- **Selection scoring** — audience scrape, content-credibility
  analysis. Production-ready.
- **Content variant generation** — captured footage to environmental
  variants. Production-with-guardrails.
- **Interview prompt drafting** — question sets for podcast or
  feature interviews. Production-ready.
- **Race-recap content** — athlete result to draft recap.
  Production-with-guardrails.

Where AI doesn't help and shouldn't try:

- **Generating new "athlete" footage** of scenes that didn't happen.
  Even if technically possible, the audience reaction when the
  fabrication is discovered destroys the partnership. This is the
  bright line in the contract.
- **Synthesising the athlete's voice** without separate consent. Even
  with consent, disclose.
- **Producing content the athlete hasn't seen.** The athlete's name
  is on it. They get the final review.

## The eval harness

**Eval A1 — Selection rubric calibration.** Backtest the rubric
against 5 previous partnerships (3 that worked, 2 that didn't).
Pipeline scores should correlate with the actual outcome at >0.7.

**Eval A2 — Contract clause coverage.** Every signed contract gets
reviewed by legal counsel for AI augmentation language. Contracts
without the AI section are blocked from signing. Non-negotiable.

**Eval A3 — Cadence sustainability.** Monthly check on each athlete:
how many pieces actually shipped vs the cadence target. If actuals
are running 50% below target, the cadence is unrealistic for that
athlete; renegotiate, don't enforce.

**Eval A4 — Audience response.** Quarterly: engagement on athlete-
branded content vs the athlete's organic content. If branded
engagement is consistently <50% of organic, the audience isn't
responding to the partnership. Surface in the renewal review.

## The failure modes

**Signing for audience size, not audience fit.** A 500k-follower
athlete whose audience doesn't overlap with the brand's segments
produces dramatically less than a 50k-follower athlete who's
exactly aligned. Selection rubric should weight audience alignment
over raw size.

**Athletes signed without content-rights clarity.** Brand wants to
re-cut footage 18 months later, the contract doesn't allow it.
Resolve in the contract, not in the production review.

**Performative partnership.** Athlete posts the contracted four
posts a month, they look like ads, audience tunes out. Reduce
cadence, increase substance per piece.

**Athlete behaviour catches the brand off-guard.** Public falling-
out, positive doping test, controversial public stance. Have an
exit clause and rehearse using it.

**Co-marketing without coordination.** Athlete is sponsored by three
brands, all running parallel campaigns. Audience sees a Christmas-
tree of logos. Either negotiate exclusivity bands or accept the
shared-spotlight reality and brief accordingly.

**Race-day "congratulations" template.** Same template every race.
Audience pattern-matches it as automated. Replace with one specific
observation per race — what changed in the athlete's performance,
what the race meant in their season, what conditions they overcame.

## The pattern in practice

Illustrative scenarios — common shapes ambassador programmes take. Specifics are illustrative; the patterns repeat.

**Premium cycling brand, scale-stage — the taper unlock.** A brand
with eight ambassadors, substantial annual spend, no articulable
return. Rebuilding the roster around the selection rubric typically
tapers to a smaller named group with substantially higher fit.
Total spend drops. Engagement on athlete content multiplies — not
because each athlete is suddenly stronger, but because the four
remaining are real audience matches and the noise of the other
four was suppressing the signal.

**Running brand, growth-stage — the wrong-tier audience.** A brand
signing fast-time elite athletes whose audiences are primarily
other fast-time athletes — but the brand's actual customer is an
intermediate runner who can't relate to 2:25 marathoners. Re-
focusing signings toward 2:50–3:30 marathoners with strong
intermediate-runner audiences and relatable personal narratives
typically lifts newsletter conversion from athlete-introduced
content multiple times over. Audience-fit beats raw performance
in ambassador economics.

**Triathlon brand — the contract-clause-that-wasn't.** A common
historical failure: signing athletes who score well on the rubric
but with contracts that don't include AI augmentation language.
Eighteen months in, the brand wants to extend a hero shoot with
AI variants for the next season and finds the contracts don't
cover it. One athlete declines; the other grants permission for
an unbudgeted fee. This is why AI-augmentation rights are now
table-stakes contract language — every contract carries the
clause from day one.
