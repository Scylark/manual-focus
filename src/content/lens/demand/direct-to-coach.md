## The brief

Endurance buyers ask their coach what shoe / bike / nutrition / kit
to use. Coaches recommend. The brands that recognise this and build
a direct channel to coaches earn audience access that no paid
campaign can match. The brands that don't — almost all of them —
treat coaches as marketing afterthoughts.

This playbook is the discipline of the coach channel. Tier menu by
coach size and influence, sample-distribution model that's
generous without being mercenary, attribution framework that
distinguishes coach-driven sales from organic, and — most
importantly — the language coaches actually respond to (which is
not the language consumers respond to).

It is not a referral / affiliate programme. Coaches don't want
discount codes; they want products that work for their athletes and
brands that respect their expertise.

## The pipeline

Six phases.

**Phase 1 — Coach landscape map.** Identify the coaching
populations relevant to the brand:

- **Individual coaches** — independent coaches with small private
  rosters (5–30 athletes each). Often the most credible recommenders.
- **Coaching collectives** — multi-coach businesses (e.g.
  TrainingPeaks coaches, regional triathlon coaching practices)
- **Club coaches** — coaches affiliated with running clubs, cycling
  clubs, triathlon squads. Often unpaid or low-paid; rely on
  goodwill.
- **Online platform coaches** — coaches running their service via
  Strava, TrainingPeaks Premium, Final Surge, etc.
- **Federation / national-team coaches** — small group, high
  influence, hard to reach
- **Influencer-coaches** — coaches with public followings
  (YouTube, Instagram, podcast). Different relationship dynamic;
  these are closer to ambassadors than coaches.

Build the database of named coaches in the brand's target geographies
+ disciplines. Most brands underestimate this number; in a single
discipline, a country typically has 200–2,000 coaches worth tracking.

**Phase 2 — Tier menu.** Three tiers, by coach influence and brand
engagement:

- **Tier A — Partner coaches.** Active relationship. The brand
  provides product for their personal use, samples for their
  athletes (within reasonable counts), and educational access
  (clinics, product walk-throughs, athlete-feedback channels).
  Expects: the coach uses and recommends the product genuinely
  when fit; honest feedback to the brand on what's working.
- **Tier B — Network coaches.** On the brand's list. Receives:
  product information, periodic outreach, sample-programme access
  on application. Lighter touch, scalable.
- **Tier C — Database.** Known coaches the brand hasn't built a
  relationship with. Receive: brand newsletter, opt-in product
  announcements. Top of funnel for Tier B promotion.

**Phase 3 — Sample distribution programme.** The hardest balance:
generous enough that coaches feel respected, structured enough
not to become a free-product programme.

Recommended model:

- Tier A coaches: full product seeding annually, plus 2–5 samples
  per quarter for their athletes (the brand fulfils samples
  requested by Tier A coaches at no charge, within sensible limits)
- Tier B coaches: an annual product-sample allowance on application
  (e.g. one product per year, 3 athlete samples per quarter on
  request)
- Sample requests routed through a simple form (athlete name,
  product needed, why this fit) — light enough to be friction-free,
  detailed enough to spot abuse

Cost per sample is real money; the budget needs to be sized against
the LTV uplift. Most brands underbudget the sample programme and
choke the channel.

**Phase 4 — Educational content for coaches.** What coaches want
from the brand isn't ads. It's:

- **Product knowledge** — fit guides, comparison vs competitors,
  technical detail their athletes will ask about
- **Athlete-recovery / training research** the brand has invested in
- **Early-access** to product launches so the coach is informed
  before their athletes ask
- **Coach community moments** — small-group product clinics,
  athlete-recovery roundtables, fitting workshops

The content is delivered through a dedicated coach channel — email
list, private community, periodic in-person events. Treat it
distinctly from consumer marketing.

**Phase 5 — Attribution.** The hard part. Coaches don't carry
discount codes; their recommendations don't tag.

Three attribution layers:

- **Direct attribution** — for sample-fulfilment requests, the
  brand knows which coach requested for which athlete. When that
  athlete subsequently purchases, the brand can credit the coach.
- **Survey attribution** — at checkout, optional "who recommended
  us?" question. Coaches mentioned often surface as channel
  drivers.
- **Cohort attribution** — coaches' clients converge on certain
  brands; track concentration of clients in a coach's roster
  buying a specific product as signal that the coach is
  recommending.

None of this is perfect. The coaches programme is justified by
incremental LTV, brand reputation in coaching circles, and qualitative
feedback from new customers, not just last-click attribution.

**Phase 6 — Annual review.** Tier A coaches get an annual review
conversation: what worked, what didn't, was the sample programme
appropriate, would they recommend the brand to other coaches. The
relationship matters; the conversation maintains it.

## The capability boundary

What AI helps with:

- **Coach database management** — building the map, classifying
  coaches by sport / region / size
- **Personalised outreach** — per-coach emails referencing their
  athletes' recent races, their training platform of choice
- **Educational content drafting** — fit guides, comparison docs,
  training-research summaries
- **Sample request triage** — surfacing suspicious patterns (one
  coach requesting 50 samples in a quarter is a red flag)

What AI doesn't help with:

- **The relationship.** Tier A coach relationships are human work.
  AI augments the outreach; humans build the trust.
- **Judging the coach's credibility.** A human salesperson with
  category knowledge calls.

## The eval harness

**Eval C1 — Tier A coverage.** Are Tier A coaches active across the
brand's priority disciplines and geographies? Gaps flagged for
recruitment.

**Eval C2 — Sample-to-purchase conversion.** Of athletes who received
samples through a coach, what percentage purchase the product (or
adjacent product) within 90 days? Target: 25%+ for Tier A coach
samples, indicating the coach is recommending well.

**Eval C3 — Coach-survey attribution.** Percentage of new customers
naming a coach at checkout. The trend matters more than the
absolute number — climbing share signals the channel working.

**Eval C4 — Channel cost per customer.** Total programme cost
(samples, education, events, headcount) divided by attributable
customer acquisitions. Below the brand's blended CAC = the channel
is paying back.

## The failure modes

**Treating coaches like influencers.** Coaches are not paid to post.
A "we'll give you free product if you do an Instagram review" pitch
insults serious coaches; they're not in the influencer game. Build
the programme around their athletes' needs, not the brand's social
KPIs.

**Sample-programme abuse.** A small number of "coaches" will sign
up to receive free product without serving real athletes. Light
verification at signup (link to coaching profile, list of named
athletes, training platform credentials) screens out most.

**Pricing favours direct.** Brand offers consumer pricing that's
better than what shops or coaches can match. Coaches stop
recommending because they look stupid recommending a brand the
customer can buy cheaper elsewhere. Protect the channel pricing.

**Brand-led product changes that hurt coaches' athletes.** Brand
changes the shoe last; coaches' athletes spent the season fitting the
old version. The brand needs to communicate product changes to Tier A
coaches before public announcement — they get to brief their athletes
ahead of the change.

**Coach-channel content treated as consumer marketing.** Coaches
unsubscribe. The channel decays. Coach communications need a
different register, different cadence, different content.

## The pattern in practice

Illustrative scenarios — common shapes a coach-channel programme takes. Specifics are illustrative; the patterns repeat.

**Premium running brand, scale-stage — the build-from-zero.** A
brand with no existing coach programme. Building one around the
playbook over 12 months typically lands at a few dozen Tier A
coaches across the target geographies and several hundred Tier B
coaches on the list. Sample-to-purchase conversion in Tier A
typically runs around a third. Channel attribution at checkout
(coach name mentioned) climbs from negligible into double-digit
percentages within a year. LTV of coach-channel customers runs
materially above brand average.

**Triathlon brand, growth-stage — the elite-to-coach reallocation.**
A brand sponsoring elite athletes whose audiences are other elite
athletes — but the audience the brand wants is age-group triathletes
served by independent coaches. Reallocating elite-sponsorship slots
to a coach-partner programme typically lifts channel attribution
into double-digit percentages within two seasons.

**Cycling brand — the no-sample-budget failure.** A brand wants a
coach programme but refuses to budget sample fulfilment. Tier A
coaches sign up, expect samples for their athletes, don't receive
them, the channel goes cold within months. The lesson: sample
budget is the programme. Without it, there is no programme.

## Hand-off

The coach channel connects to:
- **ambassador-programme** — Tier A coaches are sometimes the right
  ambassador signing
- **training-content-engine** — coach-channel content extends from
  the brand's training POV
- **retail-partner-programme** — coaches often drive customers to
  shops; align messaging
- **lifecycle-journey-builder** — post-purchase, customers acquired
  through coaches get a coach-acknowledging touchpoint
